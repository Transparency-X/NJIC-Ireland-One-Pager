The Problem

Fragmented Oversight, Systemic Blind Spots

Ireland has no independent, cross-cutting body to detect integrity failures that span multiple justice agencies. Oversight exists in silos — and the gaps between them are where systemic risks fester.

Agency Currently Overseen By The Gap
An Garda Síochána PCSA, GSOC / IOPO No cross-system pattern analysis
Irish Prison Service Inspector of Prisons No corruption-risk benchmarking
Courts Service Judicial Council No external transparency scoring
Probation Service Internal / Ministerial No independent integrity monitoring
Legal Profession Legal Services Regulatory Authority No justice-sector-wide accountability
Result: Corruption risks, procurement irregularities, and systemic misconduct patterns that span multiple agencies go undetected until they become crises.
What the NJIC Is

A Cross-System Integrity Commission

The NJIC is designed to fill the gaps between existing oversight bodies — not replace them. It provides the connective tissue that turns fragmented data into systemic insight.

01 / Independence
A Statutory or Statutory-Style Commission
Independent of the agencies it monitors; funded through the Oireachtas or EU anti-corruption frameworks, with operational autonomy guaranteed.
02 / Scope
Cross-System Coverage
Covers An Garda Síochána, Irish Prison Service, Courts Service, Probation Service, and the legal profession — as a single, interconnected ecosystem.
03 / Focus
Integrity-Focused Benchmarking
Benchmarks transparency, ethical conduct, accountability, and corruption risk — not day-to-day operational management or performance metrics.
04 / Method
Evidence-Based & Peer-Reviewed
Publishes annual integrity scores, risk heatmaps, and policy recommendations derived from open data, FOI responses, and validated official sources.
05 / Impact
Advocacy-Enabling
Provides researchers, journalists, TDs, and civil society with structured, citable datasets to support evidence-based reform campaigns.
06 / Protection
Whistleblower-Protective
Maintains secure, anonymised channels for reporting cross-cutting integrity concerns without storing identities or attribution data.
What the NJIC Is Not

Clear Boundaries, Defined Limits

The NJIC is not a law enforcement body, a court, or an operational manager. It is an integrity monitor — and its power lies in evidence, not enforcement.

✕
A Police Force
It has no powers of arrest, search, or seizure. It does not conduct criminal investigations.
✕
A Court or Tribunal
It does not determine guilt, impose sanctions, or adjudicate individual cases.
✕
An Operational Manager
It does not set policing priorities, prison budgets, court schedules, or probation targets.
✕
A Replacement for Existing Bodies
It does not duplicate the PCSA, GSOC/IOPO, the Inspector of Prisons, or the Judicial Council.
✕
A Prosecutorial Body
It does not bring criminal charges, disciplinary proceedings, or professional sanctions.
✕
A Leaks Platform
It does not publish unverified allegations. All data is sourced, documented, and subject to provenance logging.
Existing Bodies

Complementary, Not Competitive

The NJIC uses existing oversight bodies' published outputs as inputs for its cross-system analysis. It fills the gap between single-agency oversight and whole-system integrity.

Existing Body What It Does What NJIC Adds
PCSA Garda performance oversight, inspections, community safety Cross-system integrity benchmarking; corruption-risk analysis spanning Garda-Prison-Court nexus
GSOC / IOPO Investigates individual Garda complaints Identifies systemic misconduct patterns across agencies; complements but does not investigate
Inspector of Prisons Prison conditions, human rights compliance Prison-specific integrity scoring: corruption, procurement, staff conduct
Judicial Council Judicial conduct, appointments, education External transparency index for judicial accountability
C&AG Public financial audit Sector-specific procurement risk mapping
DPP Prosecution decisions Prosecution transparency metrics: consistency, disclosure compliance
Key Principle: The NJIC relies on PCSA outputs as inputs for its Garda scoring. Conversely, the NJIC can flag systemic integrity risks that the PCSA's narrower operational focus might miss. The relationship is one of information-sharing and methodological coordination — not duplication.
The Ask

Establish the NJIC

A statutory National Justice Integrity Commission — or a formally recognised independent framework — to monitor, report, recommend, and protect across Ireland's entire justice ecosystem.

  1. 1

    Monitor

    Assess integrity risks across all justice agencies using a standardised 5-dimension methodology: Operational Effectiveness, Public Trust, Transparency, Ethical Conduct, and Resource Efficiency.

  2. 2

    Report

    Publish annual integrity reports to the Oireachtas and the public with scored, benchmarked, peer-reviewed findings — transparent, citable, and independently verified.

  3. 3

    Recommend

    Deliver evidence-based reforms to legislation, regulation, and agency practice — informed by EU best practice and international anti-corruption standards.

  4. 4

    Protect

    Safeguard whistleblowers who expose cross-system integrity failures through secure, anonymised channels that never store identities or attribution data.
